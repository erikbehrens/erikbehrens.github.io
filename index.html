Profile

About me

Background, current role, and main research themes.

I am a physical oceanographer at Earth Sciences New Zealand (ESNZ), based in Wellington. My work focuses on how the Southern Ocean and Antarctic margins interact with the rest of the climate system through large-scale circulation, water-mass formation, and the global overturning circulation.

I use a combination of global and regional ocean models, observational datasets, and increasingly machine learning to understand drivers, mechanisms, and predictability of marine extremes, including marine heatwaves and sea-level change, with a particular focus on Aotearoa New Zealand and the wider Southwest Pacific.

Current focus at ESNZ

Selected themes:

  • Links between Southern Ocean dynamics, Antarctic ice-ocean interaction, and the global overturning circulation
  • Drivers and predictability of marine heatwaves around New Zealand and the Tasman Sea
  • Regional sea-level change, extremes, and coastal risk in the Southwest Pacific
  • High-resolution ocean modelling for the NZ Exclusive Economic Zone and Antarctic gateways
  • Coupling process-based models with data-driven emulators for rapid scenario exploration
  • Co-design of tools with stakeholders across science, policy, and marine industries
Research & Projects

Selected work

A sample of projects illustrating my approach to physical oceanography and climate impact assessment.

Marine heatwaves

Forecasting coastal marine heatwave risk

Developing high-resolution forecasts and early-warning indicators for marine heatwaves around Aotearoa New Zealand and the Tasman Sea, integrating regional ocean models, reanalysis products, and machine-learning based prediction systems co-designed with end users.

Keywords: seasonal prediction, coastal impacts, aquaculture & fisheries

Sea level & circulation

Southern Ocean dynamics and global overturning

Quantifying how Southern Ocean winds, eddies, and deep convection shape the global overturning circulation and influence regional sea-level change and extremes in the Southwest Pacific and along the New Zealand coast, including teleconnections to Antarctic ice-sheet change.

Methods: global & regional ocean models, reanalysis, altimetry, process-based experiments

Data & ML

Emulators for ocean-climate extremes

Building fast, data-driven emulators of complex ocean model output ‚Äì including Southern Ocean and overturning circulation metrics ‚Äì to explore uncertainty and enable rapid scenario analysis of marine extremes and compound events, bridging high-end modelling and practical decision support.

Tools: Python, xarray, ML frameworks, HPC and cloud workflows

Internal waves & mixing

Internal tides and mixing over submarine ridges

Investigating how internal tides and internal waves generated by interaction of barotropic tides with rough topography drive enhanced mixing, modify stratification, and influence circulation pathways in the New Zealand region, including work within NIWA‚Äôs i-MIX (Internal Mixing) project.

Focus: internal tides, small-scale mixing, energy pathways, implications for ecosystems
